How to Become an Explorer and Help Save the Planet
by
Llewellyn M. Toulmin

Have you ever read National Geographic Magazine and wished that you could become an explorer or wildlife conservationist like the people you read about? Think it's impossible? Well, consider the case of Robert E. Hyman, explorer, photographer, adventurer, conservationist and mountain climber, born right here in Montgomery County, who is shaking up the world of ornithology in Central America with his recent discoveries.


Robert Hyman's Honduran Expedition
According to Hyman, "We have been working hard to document and preserve the habitat of the Honduran Emerald Hummingbird, one of the most endangered bird species in Central America, and one of the most endangered hummingbirds on earth. I just returned from a very successful expedition to Honduras, in which we found a few remaining examples of the bird in western Honduras, where the bird had been thought to be extinct and not seen for over 70 years. This is a major rediscovery which will hopefully lead to a substantial conservation effort and more scientific research."

Robert described the expedition in detail. "For one week in November of 2008 we conducted searches in the Honduran departments of Santa Barbara and Cort's, and were able to find six sites inhabited by the Emerald, all in the department of Santa Barbara. Our searches were directed by over-flights of the same area in February of 2007 and a sighting recently reported to our group by local experts Mario Espinal and Leonel Marineros. We found the Emerald in patches of forest measuring 5 to 60 hectares along a 33-kilometer-long transect. As in northeastern Honduras, its remaining habitat is highly fragmented. Finding the species in western Honduras gives us hope for the conservation of the species, because the discovery increases both the known distributional range and population size of the species. However, due to the highly fragmented nature of its habitat, the species definitely warrants its status as critically endangered."

The Honduran Emerald hummingbird gets its name from its gorgeous, glittering blue-green throat and upper chest. There are only an estimated 500 to 2000 breeding pairs left in the wild. It is the only bird species that is endemic to Honduras. According to Hyman, "It's ironic that the bird has no major natural predators except for man, who is destroying the Emerald's habitat at a tremendous rate. The hummingbird beats its wings so fast that it must feed every 15 minutes, from nectar producing flowers. These flowers grow only in certain isolated patches of dry thorn forest that are rapidly being destroyed by highway construction, cattle grazing and local agriculture."

The Emeralds are lucky to have such a champion from Montgomery County, for Robert Hyman is a certainly a remarkable person. He is quick to avow that "I am not a scientist, and I don't have an advanced degree. But I have always been interested in exploration and adventure, so I have sought out interesting opportunities."

That is certainly a major understatement. Robert's interesting experiences include:
- Participating in the first exploratory and mountaineering team into Albania in 50 years, immediately after the fall of Albania's crazed dictator Enver Hoxha.
- Finding evidence of an ancient civilization in the remote Darien Gap region of Panama.
- Leading 24 climbers and explorers in a search for downed explorer and aviator Steve Fossett.
- Climbing 48 of the 50 state highpoints, as part of his membership in The Highpointers Club, where he serves on the board.
- Kayaking on the world's highest navigable lake in Bolivia
- Participating in a high altitude medical study on Mount Everest.

Robert is a past chairman of The Explorers Club Washington Group, and was recently made a Fellow of the Royal Geographical Society. He thinks that many more people could do what he has done. "Exploration and conservation don't have to be super-expensive. A lot of my expeditions cost about the same as a vacation and take about as long: 10 days to two weeks. We often save money by staying in cheap accommodations or in tents, and we eat what the locals eat. The key thing is that we are working on something important."

Hyman added, "For example, through our research on the Honduran Emerald, we have developed enough scientific information to help petition the US Department of the Interior for protection for the hummingbird under the Endangered Species Act. Such protection will make it easier for the Honduran and US governments, non-government organizations (NGOs), and local Hondurans to work together to protect the bird."

The main threat to the bird is habitat destruction, and Hyman's efforts may help considerably in that area. He said, "Our goal is to get the Honduran government, utilities, NGOs, and the US government to assist with purchasing or reserving the land on which the remaining habitat is located. Also, we are helping to advise local developers and land owners on how to mitigate their activities so as to preserve the habitat rather than destroy it. The Hondurans want to help, because the Emerald is a symbol of their country, and they want to see it preserved. Our rediscovery of the Emerald in parts of the country where it was previously thought to be extinct, was big news in the birding community."

Hyman described a typical day on his recent Emerald expedition: "We get up every morning early, about 4:00 am, because the bird is most active at dawn. We drive about 40 minutes to the nearest outcrop of correct thorn brush habitat, and quietly set up observation posts. We play recordings or make imitations of the only bird that is the enemy of the Emerald. That is the Ferruginous Pygmy Owl, which does not eat the hummingbirds, but which sometimes steals eggs from their nests. When the Emeralds hear the call of the owl, they come and aggressively try to protect their territory and scare it away. Those Emeralds are fierce little fighters. Then if we are lucky, we will get a good photo of the bird, document the habitat site and get a count of how many Emeralds were seen at that location."

Hyman's recent expedition was not without incident. He said, "We had parked our Expedition vehicles on a trail into the woods, off the main road. For security we left Leonel with the two vehicles, a beat-up truck with US AID [Agency for International Development] markings, and a much fancier, tinted-window SUV. Leonel got nervous when he spotted a guy on a motorbike slowly passing by, on the main road, for a second time. The guy was staring at our vehicles and using his cell phone. Leonel thought he was about to be robbed, and just then our team emerged from the forest. Suddenly a bunch of jeeps rushed up the trail with the motorcycle man, and guys jumped out dressed in plain clothes and holding automatic weapons and hand guns. We all thought we were in real trouble. But it turned out that it was the local police SWAT team. They had thought we were acting suspiciously, parked along the side of the road for so long, and that we were narco-traffickers in the fancy SUV robbing an AID worker in the beat-up truck. Meanwhile, we thought they were robbing us! So we all had a good laugh, and now we're best buddies."

Robert has some advice for would-be explorers. "The Explorers Club has Associate and Student membership categories for persons who have not yet undertaken major explorations and published about them. There is more information about the Club, which was founded in 1904 and includes some of the world's most famous mountaineers, divers, aviators, astronauts and scientists, at www.explorers.org. Members of the Club achieved numerous "firsts," including first to the North and South Poles, first to the deepest part of the ocean, first to the summit of Mt. Everest, and first to the Moon."
