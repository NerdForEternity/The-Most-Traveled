The Cruising World

Death of the Fish
by
Lew Toulmin

There are 830 UNESCO World Heritage Sites on land. Imagine the huge outcry from travelers, historians and the public if 700 of them were destroyed. But that is exactly what is happening under water, out of our view. Big fish and beautiful coral reefs are the big attractions there, and snorkelers, scuba divers and cruisers want to be able to imitate Jacques Cousteau and go see them. Unfortunately, it is almost too late.

My attention was drawn to this problem on a cruise through the southwest Pacific with Zegrahm Expeditions (www.zeco.com; 192 Nickerson St., #200, Seattle, WA 98109, 800-628-8747). Our Zegrahm snorkeling guide was Dr. Jack Grove, a Fellow of the Explorers Club, author of Fishes of the Galapagos, and one of the foremost experts on fish in the Pacific. He commented that finding big fish to view is very difficult, even in remote areas. He said that the number of sharks in the Pacific is down by 80 to 90 percent over the last two decades, and that numerous commercial fish have declined by 90 percent or more. He said, "Fish stocks and even fish steaks could be just a fond memory in the future."

Shocked and amazed, I queried some of the other divers and experienced snorkelers on board. They confirmed what Jack had said. Most estimated that their own personal sightings of fish over two feet long, even off remote islands in Papua New Guinea and the Solomons, had declined by 70 to 80 percent over the last 15 years. What was going on?

Back home, I did some research. I found a scientific estimate in the prestigious journal Nature, which used actual catches and computer modeling to analyze the problem. The authors stated bluntly that "industrialized fisheries typically reduce fish stocks by 80 percent within 15 years of exploitation." Specific regional findings included "a loss of 60 percent in the Gulf of Thailand of the large finfish, sharks and skates in the first five years of industrial trawl fishing" and "high declines off South Georgia island, which was effectively fished down during the first two years of exploitation." The authors concluded bleakly that "our analysis suggests that the global ocean has lost more than 90 percent of its large fish."

A similar report in the journal Science in November 2006, sponsored by the National Science Foundation, indicated that all major world fish stocks could be destroyed in terms of commercial viability, within about 30 years, at the current rate of decimation.

Who is behind this wholesale slaughter? It turns out that there are about 30 million ocean fishing vessels in the world, but only 38,400 large ones (over 100 tons). These large commercial vessels account for over 60 percent of the world's catch. They scoop up huge numbers of fish, and yet discard many fish as "trash." Their efficiency is frightening. They use spotting planes, high technology, and nets large enough to swallow twelve jumbo 747s. Destructive "bottom trawlers" use heavy nets with metal plates and rubber wheels, which crush almost all the life in their path. "Shark finners" cut the fins off of large sharks and discard the carcasses, saving the only lucrative fins for expensive shark fin soup. And many commercial fishermen ignore national boundaries, fish sanctuaries and underwater parks, and blatantly break the law in their quest for cash.

Where do these rapacious fishermen come from? Many use flags of convenience (Panama, Liberia, Bahamas, Antigua, and about 28 others) and complex ownership structures, so they are hard to trace. But the biggest fleets seem to be financed and managed from China, Taiwan and Indonesia.

What is the answer to this problem? According to expert Jack Grove, establishing Marine Reserves would be a huge help. He noted that there are very few Marine Reserves world wide, that they cover less than one percent of the ocean's area, and that many so-called reserves actually allow fishing in them. He said, "What is needed are more and larger "no take" Marine Reserves, where all fishing is prohibited." Grove emphasized that enforcement is key, and that even in reserves like the Galapagos, where he works, many fish pirates sneak in at night, take advantage of the low funding for enforcement, and capture sharks and other fish.

A study by scientists at Cambridge University in Britain in 2003 concluded that "less than one fiftieth of one percent of the waters of Britain are protected by Marine Reserves." These researchers found that establishing Marine Reserves often had fast and dramatic benefits: "Within a year or two of establishing a reserve, [fish] population densities increased 91 percent, average fish size went up 31 percent, and species diversity rose 20 percent." Establishing Marine Reserves "covering 30 percent of the world's oceans would create millions of jobs and only cost about $12 billion, less than the $15 to $30 billion that the world's governments now spend to subsidize over-fishing."

Thinking back on my own travels, I realized that I had experienced the solution without even realizing there was a problem. Several years ago on a trip to San Pedro, Belize, I went snorkeling off the beach, and spotted only a few tiny fish on the offshore coral reef. The next day I went snorkeling in the local Marine Reserve. It was small, only about three square miles. But the difference was incredible. The fish were large, beautiful and colorful. I could swim up close to them, take pictures, and study their fascinating behavior. I felt like Jacques Cousteau. It was paradise.
