Chasing Cheese on a Cruise Through France
by
Llewellyn Toulmin

Charles de Gaulle once famously said, ďHow can anyone be expected to govern a country with 325 cheeses?Ē Luckily for us, France is now even more ungovernable, with over 700 types of cheese to sample and enjoy. As a passenger on an August 2009 cruise on the Burgundy Canal aboard French Country Waterways Horizon II, I picked the brain of FCWís cheese and wine expert Emma Dumel (www.fcwl.com; 800-222-1236, 1-781-934-2454; PO Box 2195, Duxbury, MA 02331 USA). She said, ďTo really experience France, you must learn about French cheese. That is why we serve 27 varieties of cheese in a week of cruising.Ē


Emma Dumel of French Country Waterways on Horizon II
If you are from the US, sample your cheeses in France rather than the US, because according to Emma, ďmany French soft cheeses are made from unpasteurized milk, and cannot be shipped to the US, since they do not have an Ďaffinageí or aging process of over 60 days.Ē Emma contended that, ďI have never heard of any problems with unpasteurized French cheeses, and after all, the French have been happily eating them for hundreds of years.Ē According to the authoritative French Cheese Book by Patrick Rance (Macmillan, 1989), ďscientific tests and tastings have shown the pasteurization destroys the aromatic Ďesters,í the vital sources of aroma and flavor, conducted by plant oils into the body fat and mammary glands of the dairy cow (or sheep or goat), and thence into the resulting milk.Ē If you are from the UK, you are luckier. According to Sarah Duggan of Nealís Yard Dairy in London, ďIt is possible for UK citizens to buy French cheeses made from unpasteurized milk in the UK, but they can be hard to find except in London. Also, it is legal for UK citizens to purchase any French cheese in France and bring it back to the UK, for personal consumption.Ē

Once in France, you should focus mainly on the ďartisanalĒ cheeses rather than the ďindustriel,Ē ďcooperativeĒ or ďfermierĒ cheeses. The artisanal cheeses are carefully made in small batches, while the industriel and cooperative are factory-scale productions, and the fermier are tiny individual productions that are not readily available. Said Emma, ďgo to cheese stalls in weekly town markets, cheese shops, and even supermarkets like Carrefour and Super U, which will have large cheese counters and knowledgeable staff.Ē Emma continued, ďAt these establishments, donít hesitate to ask for five or six slivers of different cheeses to taste. The minimum amount to buy is usually 100 or 150 gramsĒ [about 3.5 to 5.2 ounces]. Emma gave out a little secret: ďOn Horizon II we buy all our cheese at Les Fromages de Sylvain.Ē [1 Grand Rue, Flogny la Chapelle in Burgundy; tel. 03-86-75-59-74, www.lesfromagesdesylvain.fr; this firm has a stall at weekly markets in Burgundy at the towns of Tonnerre and Chablis on Saturday and Sunday, respectively.]

Learn to pick your favorite types of cheese by eye, differentiating the hard from the soft, the dry from the creamy, and the goat (ďchevresĒ) from the ewes (ďbarbisĒ) and the cow (ďvachesĒ) or the mixed milk (ďlait de mťlangeĒ). Emma noted that ďthe famous AOC label is a guarantee of quality Ė it stands for ĎAppelation díOrigine ContrŰlťeí and is a French certificate used in wine, cheese, bottled water and other products, which ensures that specified methods of production were used in a certain region.Ē Any violation by manufacturers of the AOC rules carries a fine and a jail term of up to a year, so this label is taken quite seriously.


A round of Epoisses Berthaut cheese
However, there are only 45 AOC cheeses, and hundreds of non-AOC types, so donít confine your quest to the few AOCs. For example, there are at least 25 types of the famous Brie cheese, but only two, Brie de Meaux and Brie de Melun, have the AOC label. The former was tasted by Charlemagne in 772, while the latter probably dates back to Roman times. Both of these are made from unpasteurized milk in the central őll de France region, and both are 45 percent fat, yet the former is described as having a slight smell of mould, sweet and smoky, with a rich, condensed flavor, while the latter by contrast is strong, salty and robust. So even cheeses with almost identical names and make-ups can have quite different tastes.

Sample some of the classic cheeses that are generally available nation-wide, such as Brie, Cantal, Comptť, Emmantal, Camenbert, …poisses de Bourgogne, Roquefort and Abondance. Then narrow your quest, based on your preferences. For example, suppose you like the amazingly soft, pungent and milky …poisses de Bourgogne (a favorite of Napoleon, which he paired with Chambertin wine). Then you might proceed next to try the similar but harder-to-find Affidelice, also made in …poisses, Burgundy, but washed 1-3 times per day in Chablis wine instead of in marc de Bourgogne.

Prices for classic cheeses in a supermarket can vary from 3 euros (about $4.20) for 150 grams for a Brie, to 10 euros for 150 grams of an aged Comptť. Rarer artisanal cheeses vary hugely in price, from a few euros per 150 grams up to 6 euros for a Delice de Bourgogne (double cream cowís milk) or 15 euros for a pungent Maroilles (cowís milk cheese from Picardy in northeast France, soft and golden, first made by a monk in 962). You can score some bargains by buying local cheeses. For example, a 250 gram President Petit Camembert made in Normandy and purchased locally in a supermarket there cost only 1.56 euros ($2.18), and a locally made 250 gram Rťo Camembert with AOC label cost only 2.90 euros ($4.06).

When the magic moment comes to serve your selected cheese, Emma recommended that, ďmost cheeses should be left at room temperature for at least 30 minutes before serving, to maximize their bouquet. But some cheeses like the light, frothy Brillat-Savarin, can get too runny after just 45 minutes at room temperature. Keep your cheeses refrigerated in the wax paper they are sold in, and donít use plastic cling wrap.Ē

A great way to save money while touring in France is to picnic with the ďHoly TrinityĒ of food: local cheese, bread and wine. Round this out with a little local pate or sausage, and at todayís restaurant prices you can save 25-30 euros per person per meal. For breakfast, save 15-20 euros per person off the rip-off hotel restaurant prices by eating a nice meal in your room of fromage blanc (similar to yogurt), mixed with raisins, whipped cream and honey.

Eating cheese probably dates back to the domestication of sheep and goats, 10,000 years ago. Hopefully mankind will still be visiting France and chasing hundreds of cheese sensations, 10,000 years from now!
