The Cruising World

Tips on Snorkeling
Part 1
by
Lew Toulmin

Many cruise and expedition passengers enjoy snorkeling off beaches, Zodiacs or dive boats. Snorkeling is an easy sport, and provides fantastic views of beautiful coral and fish. The sport is open to swimmers of any age, and I have seen snorkelers from age 5 to 85 enjoying themselves on cruises. I myself thought the sport was so easy that I knew it all. Then I met master snorkeler and diver Dr. Jack Grove, expert on the fish of the Galapagos, Fellow of the Explorers Club, and one of the founders of Zegrahm Expeditions (www.zeco.com; 192 Nickerson St., # 200, Seattle, WA 98109, 800-628-8747). He gave me so many tips that my head, not just my body, was swimming. Now I can pass them on to you, to make your snorkeling experience more safe, enjoyable and accessible.

Buddy System and Safety

You will likely ride to the beach or snorkel site in a Zodiac or dive boat. Get into the boat with a "clipper grip," in which you reach past the crewman's hand to grasp his wrist. He in turn grasps your wrist. Thus if either of you lets go, you are still attached, and the chances of a fall are minimized. A clipper grip is MUCH stronger than a simple handshake grip, which should never be used. Use the clipper grip in all situations at sea or on land where support is needed or safety is an issue.

When stepping into a Zodiac, put a damp towel on the gunwale (the inflated tube that forms the side of the boat). Step onto the towel (being helped by a crewman using a clipper grip), then step lightly into the center of the Zodiac, onto the floorboards. Time your move from the parent craft to the Zodiac, dive boat, or tender, very carefully. Observe the swell and follow the crew's directions. Swells can vary from nothing to up to several feet.

When moving across the Zodiac or dive boat, keep your body weight low by crouching. When moving along the length of the Zodiac, slide your behind along the inflated tube gunwale. To exit the Zodiac in a beach landing, slide up to the front of the craft, face the rear of the vessel, away from the beach, swing one leg over the tube gunwale, then the other leg. Time your alighting so that you step on the beach between the breaking waves.

Always snorkel with a buddy, and keep an eye out for him or her. Agree on a "snorkel plan," follow it, and stay right beside each other. A typical snorkel plan could be something like: "We'll swim up into the current for 200 yards for about 20 minutes, while we're fresh, then drift down-current for 15 minutes to the take-out point, by which time we'll be tired."

The Best Type of Footwear

It may seem strange to talk about footwear, other than flippers' in snorkeling, but it is important to protect your feet from coral, and from glass on the shoreline. Leave your flip-flops, open toed sandals, and "reef walkers" at home. The first two don't protect your toes, and the latter are usually so rounded on the bottom that they make it easy to sprain your ankle. I did this in my first five minutes of wearing reef walkers in Bequia, West Indies; it ruined my whole vacation since I almost unable to walk for a week. Pick a shoe that has a wide, flat bottom, and has substantial protection for your toes and all around your feet. Wear these shoes on the beach, on the parent craft, and in the snorkel boat, until just before putting on your flippers. Running shoes that are completely made of man-made material, and thus won't rot, are suitable. Look for shoes that say "waterproof" and have drain holes and mesh sides or lots of protective straps.

For socks, get some 2 mm thick neoprene ankle booties (available at any dive shop) and wear them inside your shoes and inside your flippers. This will help keep out the sand, pebbles and most of the water, keep your feet quite comfortable, and give you more protection against fire coral.

Flippers provided by on-site operators are usually sufficient for casual snorkelers, and thus you won't have to lug awkward flippers on the plane. However, you may wish to bring your own fitted flippers if you have bunions or oddly shaped or sized feet. Or you can purchase small swim fins, only about 3 inches longer than your feet, and bring them along on the trip.

When snorkeling off a beach, carry your flippers into the water with you. Sit down in waist deep water on the sand, put on your flippers, and swim away. To get out, reverse the process. Don't try to walk on the beach or in shallow water with your flippers on, you could easily fall.

In a dive boat, put your flippers on last. Then slide gently over the side into the water. To get out of the water, hold on to the ladder off the side of the Zodiac or boat, take off your flippers one at a time, holding each one tightly, and place each one in the bottom of the boat. Then climb up the ladder into the boat.

Snorkels and Snorkeling Techniques

An inexpensive, $15 to $25 snorkel is sufficient for the casual user. But buy your own and take it on the trip. A snorkel supplied by a local operator may not have been disinfected, and a previous user may have had a bad cold. Get a snorkel that is curved, with a bend near your mouth so that it doesn't pull on your mouth or on the facemask head strap. The snorkel should have a clip that attaches to the head strap, so that the snorkel doesn't flop around without support. Make sure that the clip attaches securely to the facemask strap, but is detachable without having to pull the facemask strap out of the corner of the facemask. When using the snorkel, don't bite down on it, just hold it firmly in your mouth.

While snorkeling, don't touch any corals or fish. Swim at least six feet above any coral heads below. (But be aware that objects look closer under water, so what you thought was six feet could be eight to ten.) Don't drift backwards, you might run into some coral. Swim with a life vest or a "noodle" if you are at all nervous about your swimming ability. Stay away from jelly fish, some of which have long, almost invisible tentacles. Stay away from all boat propellers, even if they are not turning.

For the best views of those glorious fish and coral reefs, look "down sun" (with the sun behind you). But don't cast your shadow over the object you're viewing. Float flat in one spot for a while, and study what's going on below. Get a local fish field guidebook, and learn to recognize some of the fish you are seeing. Study fish behavior such as territoriality, camouflage, feeding, "cleaning stations" (where little fish clean the gills of big fish), mating and reproduction.

Learn some of the snorkelers sign language for underwater communication. The one you'll use the most is three fingers thrust forward like a trident. It means "W" and stands for "Wow!"
