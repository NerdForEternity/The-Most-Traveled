Travel Tales

M.V. Discovery Circumnavigates
the Black Sea
by
Llewellyn Toulmin


Detail of a diorama of seige of Sevastopol
The Black Sea region is a land of legend and history, where the Great Flood likely occurred, Jason and the Argonauts searched for the Golden Fleece, the Light Brigade charged into the Valley of Death, the Yalta Conference carved up the world, and so much more. Surprisingly, very few cruise ships visit this storied area, so my wife Susan and I were fortunate in October 2010 to be guests aboard M.V. Discovery's circumnavigation of the Black Sea, calling at Istanbul, Trabzon (Turkey), Sochi (Russia), Yalta, Sevastapol and Odessa (Ukraine), Nessebur island (Bulgaria) and back to Istanbul. (Contact: Voyages of Discovery (VOD), www.voyagesofdiscovery.com, 1810 SE 10th Ave., Suite 205, Ft. Lauderdale, FL 33316, 866-623-2689.)

We joined the ship in Istanbul, after VOD Reservations Manager Rhonda Suarez had saved our bacon by straightening out a mix-up with Turkish Airlines, who thought our tickets had not been issued and were demanding additional last minute payment. Reacting within minutes late on a Sunday evening, Rhonda made it clear that the e-tickets had been paid for and issued. Impressive!

Aboard the ship, we found our cabin (number 3107 on the Coral Deck) was attractive, about 8 x 15 feet, with a 6 x 4 foot bathroom en suite with shower. The recently refurbished cabin had off-white walls, British hunting prints, blue wall-to-wall carpeting, two single beds which could be pushed together to form a king bed, two 13-inch portholes, individual AC/heat, closet and tan wood trim.

Roaming the ship, we found the famous curving stairway in front of reception where scenes from "The Love Boat" TV series were filmed after the launch of the ship in 1972. Remarkably, this TV series, which aired from 1977 to 1986, really jump-started the modern cruising industry. It was quite a thrill to stand where Captain Merrill Stubing (Gavin MacLeod), Cruise Director Julie McCoy (Lauren Tewes) and all the other kooky characters interacted. Discovery, then known as Island Princess, served as a backup film set to the Pacific Princess, which was the main filming location.

We explored the excellent on-board library, noted the new room devoted entirely to bridge (you can compete twice a day there), the alternate dining venue The Yacht Club (no extra charge), gym (free, except for a charge of five UK pounds for yoga and Pilades), cruise shop and movie theater. We were impressed with the sound system in the movie theater, which was excellent and had been updated since we sailed on this ship about six years ago across the South Pacific. We noted that even though the 750 passenger ship was completely full for this very popular and rare cruise, it never seemed crowded except once for a Cossack singing performance in the main show theater.

We began the voyage with a dramatic nighttime transit through the winding and narrow Bosporus, linking the Sea of Marmara to the Black Sea. This vital "choke point" is where the mythic Jason and the Argonauts sailed through to the eastern shores of the Black Sea, questing for the Golden Fleece. And some archaeologists now think that this is the location of the Biblical Great Flood, when the rising Mediterranean and Marmara Seas broken through to the Black Sea and flooded a large area in just 300 days, around 5600 BC.

Highlights of the voyage were many, but three standouts were Stalin's dacha, the battleground of the Charge of the Light Brigade, and the site of the Yalta Conference. Stalin's dacha (second home) in Sochi, Russia was a perfect example of his paranoid personality. It was painted green so that snipers and dive bombers couldn't spot it, had numerous bedrooms so Stalin could sleep in a different bed each night, and all the furniture was cut down in height, to make the diminutive Stalin look bigger!

At Sevastapol we toured the Light Brigade site and other Crimean War (1854-5) locations. Fortunately the site of the Charge -- one of the most famous actions in military history -- is preserved fairly well, being covered by vineyards with little urban development. It was quite amazing to stand on the heights of the Sapoune Ridge where Lord Raglan wrote his fatal, poorly worded order to charge, and see his view of the battleground. From his vantage point, the valley below looks flat and one can understand how he blundered into thinking that his cavalry below could see British guns being captured from four redoubts. It is only when we descended to the valley below that we realized that the low Causeway Ridge prevented the cavalry (and us) from seeing the crucial redoubts, so the Light Brigade charged in the wrong direction, into massed Russian guns and Cossack cavalry. As a result of the blunder, of the 661 mounted troopers in the Brigade, 292 men and 362 horses were killed or wounded, and the Light Brigade, the finest light cavalry unit in the world, was destroyed as a fighting force.


Musician at Yalta conference site
In Yalta we saw the beautiful ex-Romanov Livadia Palace where Stalin hosted Churchill and FDR at the 1945 Yalta Conference which shaped the fate of the entire postwar world. We saw the table where the conferees met, and Discovery passengers danced with local musicians in the garden where the historic photographs of the three main conferees was taken. As my wife said, "This place gives me goose bumps."

Our fellow passengers were a mix of congenial British, US and Australian nationals, mostly retired, well-traveled couples. According to Assistant Cruise Director Louise Griffiths, "About 40 to 50 percent of our passengers are repeaters," a high percentage.

We spoke with Food and Beverage Manager Neill Sharer about the challenges of supplying fresh, tasty food in a moving location, all over the world. He said, "We have found that Hamburg is the best place to resupply our ships by sea, truck or air. We order our food four to five months in advance, sourced from all over the world, and usually send it by sea in four to five shipping containers or in a convoy of trucks. We make all our baked goods from scratch on board, and obtain lettuce, cauliflower and other greens locally in reliable ports like Malta." Neill continued, "We almost never have a problem with our system, since we've been doing this for years. But last year we mistakenly shipped only one pallet of toilet paper to Istanbul, instead of the usual eight. So later in Yalta we had to buy up 18,000 rolls of toilet paper from local shops: I think we cleaned out the town!"

We asked Assistant Cruise Director Griffiths about single fares, where the "single supplement" is often a hefty 100 percent of the individual fare. She said, "Discovery will not impose any single supplement between November and April for the 2011 and 2012 seasons." This is a remarkable value for single sailors. Griffiths also noted that, "A five or ten percent discount is also given to passengers who book future cruises while on board Discovery."

Griffiths described future plans for the cruise line. "It is probable that in the next two years All Leisure Group PLC, the parent company of VOD, will purchase another vessel, or will bring on line the Alexander von Humboldt, a 500 passenger ship currently on German charter. In any case, the Discovery will be forced by current rules to retire in seven years."

So if you want to sail aboard this delightful, historic lady who helped start modern cruising, start planning now. And if you want to circumnavigate the Black Sea, book early.

Lew Toulmin is a member of the Naval Order of the United States and has sailed on cruise ships and tall ships in every ocean. He lives in Silver Spring.
