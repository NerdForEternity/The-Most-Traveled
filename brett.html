Travel Tales

The Lost Dog Tag of Sergeant Raymond Brett
by
Lew Toulmin


Are there angels who guide our actions every day? Listen to my story, then you tell me.

My wife and I were on a cruise aboard the Clipper Odyssey through the southwest Pacific with Zegrahm Expeditions. In Papua New Guinea we visited remote Kiriwina Island, a classic South Seas island, with beautiful white sand beaches, waving palm trees, and an exotic culture.

Walking up the beach to the tiny village, I was approached by a tall local man. He reached in his pocket and produced five military “dog tags.” Speaking in good English, he said he found the tags nearby, where US soldiers were stationed in World War II. As an amateur historian I was intrigued. I chose one of the US tags at random and bought it for $20, hoping I could track down the family. I figured that all five men had died on the island.

I took up the hunt a few months later, having learned that www.ancestry.com had recently added millions of US military WW II enlistees to its databases. The dog tag only had the information: “R.G. Brett,” a serial number, and a contact “Mrs. A. Brett, 174-26 125th Ave, Jamica L Is NY” on it.

I searched the WW II enlistee database for an R.G. Brett, and quickly found a Raymond G. Brett born in Canada in 1919, who enlisted in the US Army in 1941 in New York. This seemed promising, since there were many Bretts but he was the only R.G. from that state.

Next I searched the US Census for 1930, and found a family in Queens with a Raymond G. Brett, and a father Adolphus W. Brett. The name Adolphus might match the Mrs. “A.” Brett on the dog tag.

Looking for Adolphus Brett in the ancestry.com WW II draft registrations, I found him born in 1888, living at 174-25 127th Ave., Jamaica, Queens, only two blocks from the dog tag address. Getting warmer!


I then noticed a Raymond G. Brett in the ancestry.com US Veterans Gravesites database, listed as a First Sergeant. But, amazingly, he hadn’t died in the South Pacific, as I had thought. He had lived a long life, died recently in 2006, and was buried in New Hampshire.

Checking the US Public Records Index on ancestry.com, I found that a Raymond G. Brett had lived in Swanzey, New Hampshire with his wife Dorothy Brett. The Index gave his recent age as 88, about right. No phone number was listed. So I searched on www.whitepages.com, and found an “R. Brett” with a telephone number listed in that area.

Total search time elapsed: about 80 minutes! I never left my computer.

With some trepidation, I called the number in New Hampshire. Did I have the right Dorothy Brett? Would she be pleased to hear from a total stranger about her recently deceased husband and his lost dog tag?

Dorothy immediately answered the phone. I needn’t have worried. She was delighted to hear from me, and was amazed at my story. She was eager to see the dog tag, and said, “Ray served in New Guinea and the Philippines during World War II for five years, after training in Texas. He never talked much about his war service, and I don’t know how his tag went missing on Kiriwina. We have two sons and a daughter, and I’m sure they’ll be fascinated by this story.” I mailed the dog tag to Dorothy.

There must have been an angel helping me find the tag in the first place, and then helping me locate Dorothy. I am only sorry that I could not meet Raymond in person, deliver his tag to him, and thank him for his service to our country in its time of need. But maybe I have met him--perhaps he was that angel, looking over my shoulder?
