The Cruising World

How to Use the Economic Downturn to Save Big on Your Next Cruise
by
Lew Toulmin

For years the cruise industry was a sellers market, with more eager passengers than cabins. But with the global economic crisis, passengers are staying home in droves, the cruising fleet is over-built, and sellers are desperate for your business. This is good news if you can afford to go cruising - and at some of the discounts available, you may be surprised how cheap it is. In this column I'll describe how to research your cruise and some of the unusual discounts available. Throughout I will give examples on how to use the tools involved, and have a great cruise at a great price.

Researching Your Cruise

First of all, be sure that you know what you are looking for. Don't buy a cruise just because it is 70 percent off the brochure price - make sure it is right for you and yours, in terms of the size of ship, atmosphere, degree of formality, destinations, food, entertainment and reputation.

To find this out, one of the greatest research tools today is the Internet, which gives much more up-to-date information on prices and reviews than any book can. If you are not facile with computers and the Internet, don't despair. Get your young relatives, or your local library, community center or senior citizens center, to assist you in your research. For example, when my computer goes haywire, I go to our local senior citizens center, and there for a small donation of $20 I get up to eight hours of time (!) from an expert volunteer, who has never failed me yet.

Some Internet sites that are extremely useful include: www.vacationstogo.com; www.cruisecompete.com; and www.cruisereviews.com. The first site, www.vacationstogo.com, has details on over 150 ships, and is searchable by ship, line, dates, region and ports. For example, suppose you are keen to go to the fascinating Baltic on a well regarded, traditional cruise line. You could search this website for the port of St. Petersburg, Russia, hit the button "find the deals," and notice that Holland America's Rotterdam sails there. I did this and found an ocean-view cabin on a Rotterdam voyage twelve weeks away for $1,699 per person, 62 percent off the brochure price of $4,419 for a 12 night voyage. Currently, savings of 30 to 70 percent off of brochure rates are typical for this website.

The second site, www.cruisecompete.com, allows you to search for and specify your cruise preference, then obtain quotes from up to 100 travel agents (and the website itself) who "bid" to offer you your chosen voyage at the lowest price. Bids are sent to you via email (or you can click through on the website to find their best price). I entered my target Rotterdam Baltic cruise and got six bids in two days. The best deal was the website's own price, a price of $1475 for an ocean view room, over $200 less than the even the www.vacationstogo.com price. This price of $1475 is now down to 67 percent off the brochure price nice!

The third site is www.cruisereviews.com, which provides narrative reviews of numerous ships. I found 16 reviews of the ship I am considering, Rotterdam, including some that are incredibly detailed and go on for pages about each port visited and all the major public rooms on board. One detailed review actually described the exact voyage I am researching, and so was very helpful. These reviews were generally quite positive, so I am getting pretty certain that this voyage is in my strike zone.

A site that is recommended by some cruise critics is www.cruiseopinion.com, which lists over 4800 reviews of cruise ships, with ratings in 42 categories, such as "good for families," and "quality of cruise director." However, most of the reviews on this site are over six years old, which I think is too old to be very useful. Hence I don't recommend this site except to get a general idea of a ship. The ship I am considering, Rotterdam, scores in the 90s and some 80s on most categories on this website, with the exception of "good for families," where it gets a low score of 74. But I am not taking teenagers or children along, so this does not bother me.

Discounts Available

The usual "pricing model" used for most cruises is what is called "breakthrough pricing." This complex, computerized model puts a price on each cabin in each month before the cruise at a price point designed to generate demand and deposits from buyers. The price almost always goes up as the sailing date approaches. Thus the cruise line in, say, August, can tell the passenger interested in a voyage in December that "the price will never be lower than it is now, and will almost certainly go up, so you better buy the trip right away

The key point is, that statement is only half true. It is true that you can get really great deals, especially now that cruise lines are desperate, if you book 9 to 18 months ahead. For example, in the voyage to the Baltic that I am researching, if I book 15 months ahead on Holland America instead of 3 months, I can save about 23 percent on my fare.

But if I want to sail soon, there are still ways to get around the "breakthrough" ever-increasing price model. The main ways are: book late, get specialty discounts, seasonal changes, and upgrades.

"Book late" means that as the cruise date gets very close (usually less than 30-60 days) the line will drop the prices to ensure a full sailing. If a cabin is empty as the ship departs the dock, that revenue can never be recovered by the line. So if you are flexible and can stand the uncertainty, then you should research four or five voyages you want, wait until the last minute, and then grab the best deal at a wonderful price. For example, if I am willing to dash to the Baltic now on only 11 days notice, instead of three months from now, I can get a deal via www.vacationstogo.com on the Tahitian Princess, which is sailing on a 12 day Baltic voyage for a price of just $999 for an ocean view cabin or $699 for an inside cabin. These prices are a terrific 75 and 82 percent off the brochure price, respectively.

Amazingly, note that these prices are about half of currently listed prices on the Princess Line website (www.princess.com) for the exact same voyage and cabin type. It often pays to buy from wholesalers and travel agents, not from the cruise line itself.

"Specialty discounts" means getting a discount for being over 55 years old, for being a firefighter, police officer, military person, teacher or past guest of the line, or for working for FedEx, DHL, UPS, an airline or other transport company, or for organizing a group tour. Specific discounts vary by line, and specialty retirees sometimes qualify, so check with your target line and also research your situation via your employer, union or association, and via www.vacationstogo.com, which has special sections for these discounts.

"Seasonal changes" means picking an off season or "shoulder" season to go, instead of high season. This is a great way to save and enjoy your trip more - who wants to go to southern Europe in August, for example, when it is hot, expensive, schools are out and everyone is crowding your target destinations?

"Upgrades" means that you may be able to raise the class of your cabin, shortly before sailing or once on-board, if the ship is sailing less than full. This is called a "soft sailing." Call the line about 30 days before sailing, and ask if there is a chance of an upgrade, either free or for a nominal sum. Also ask the purser as soon as you sail; there may be a few empty, better cabins due to people who literally missed the boat!

Finally, as you can tell, all of this is
very complicated. An excellent tactic is to do your research, pick out several target voyages, cabins and prices, and then call two or three travel agents who specialize in cruising. Tell them about your research and the best prices you found, and then ask them to beat your price. Go and meet with the one who is the nicest and who beats your price by the highest amount. Ask him or her to explain all the wrinkles that you might have missed. Then have a great trip, knowing you have done your homework.
