Travel Tales

Nix Your Sicks on Route 66
by
Llewellyn M. Toulmin

Oklahoma, Texas and New Mexico are tremendous states to visit on Route 66. This middle portion of the famous road has wide open spaces, amazing vistas and unusual destinations that will lift your spirits and cure your ailments.

Much of Route 66 art is kooky or kitschy:strange fiberglass giants, big commercial murals, bottle ranches and shoe trees. But Tulsa, Oklahoma is different: high art on a grand scale. The Philbrook Museum of Art is an enormous villa and gardens that looks like it was flown in from Lake Como in Italy. Established by the Phillips oil baron Waite Phillips (who also created the famous Philmont Boy Scout Ranch in New Mexico), the Tulsa museum has a great collection of European masters and Impressionists, Native American pottery and art, and an inspired offering of 19th century American art, including works by Frederic Remington, Albert Bierstadt and Thomas Moran.


The Mustang at an old Route 66 gas station
Route 66 arrows west across the panhandle of northern Texas, which we thought was going to be desolate and uninteresting. And much of the landscape is dry and flat, and many of the towns are empty or partial ghost towns. But Texas can be surprising. We discovered a canyon south of Amarillo, called "Palo Duro," known locally as "the Grand Canyon of Texas." We were stunned to find that it was gorgeous and much more approachable than the more famous Grand Canyon of Arizona. We drove down into the canyon (which you can't do in Arizona) and were awestruck by its 500 foot cliffs and surprised by its numerous water fords through intermittent streams.

That night in Amarillo, right on Route 66, we stayed at the Big Texan Restaurant and Motel. The accommodations were memorable for the four dozen live flies in our room, which rapidly became four dozen dead flies thanks to the fly swatter thoughtfully provided in each room by the management.

The food was memorable too. In the restaurant we encountered a film crew shooting a new TV show for the Travel Channel called "Man vs. Food." The rather large host, Alan Richman, travels the country taking food dares, usually involving eating a huge amount of calories in one sitting. The standard Big Texan dare is that you can eat free, if you can finish a 72 ounce steak with baked potato and dessert in one hour. If you don't finish, you must pay $50. Oh, and you get to do all this on a podium, under lights, in the middle of the restaurant, for the entertainment of the dining crowd. Alan became our new hero when he downed all the food—about five pounds total—in 33 minutes flat.

West of Amarillo we encountered some famous Route 66 art. The Cadillac Ranch was built in 1974, using classic Renaissance art implements: ten big-fin Cadillacs and a backhoe. The Caddies are stuck into the ground at a 45 degree angle, and spray paint cans are left lying on the ground for you to add your own special touch to the piece. What does it all symbolize? Existential angst? Traffic woes? The end of Western civilization? New York art critics are still arguing. If you look closely at the recent Disney movie Cars, you will see the Caddie fins forming the mountains in the distance. Doubtless they will also be featured in the Cars-land set to open in 2011 at Disney World.

Entering New Mexico, the landscape suddenly became dramatic and vertical. I had a friend who lived for years in New Mexico, and he described it as "an entirely different country." He's right: the landscape is fantastic, the people and the architecture have more of the feel of Old Mexico, and even the sunsets are richer.

In Tucumcari, New Mexico we stayed at one of the few remaining neon-signed classic car courts, the famous Blue Swallow Inn. Surprisingly, each little room, tastefully done in 1950s style, had its own small garage next door. We parked the Mustang snug for the night, right beside our room. Try that in a modern hotel!

Having lunch at a Route 66 diner in Tucumcari, we had one of those chance encounters that makes traveling on the Route so memorable. Cyril Paul, 78 years old but looking 48, was biking all the way from Minnesota to California to raise money for scholarships for boys from his native Trinidad. Each day he would buck the headwind on his bike for about 50 miles and then call his wife and supporter Pam, who would pick him up in their van, and go rest in a motel. By riding 2000 miles in 40 days he managed to raise $80,000 for the Cyril Paul West Indian Scholarship fund (http://cpwis.homestead.com/).

We traveled the 2048 miles of Route 66 in September and October, taking four weeks to drive the entire route from start to finish. We chose that time for the cooler weather, but also because we wanted to see the famous Albuquerque International Balloon Festival, which occurs each year in early October.

The Festival lived up to its star billing. Getting up before dawn, we walked to the balloon grounds and saw 650 hot air balloons lift off in just 90 minutes. They take off in waves, about 10 minutes apart. Amazingly, the tens of thousands of spectators can walk around amongst the balloons, and even help the crews stretch out the balloons on the ground, and fill them with hot air. There were hundreds of regular tear-drop shaped balloons, but the crowd favorites are always the "special shape" balloons: cartoon figures, churches, cows, fish, and stagecoaches. Even Darth Vader made an appearance as a balloon.

Revving up the Mustang, we left the Festival and headed west, towards two of the most magnificent states on Route 66: Arizona and California. But that's a story for next month.
