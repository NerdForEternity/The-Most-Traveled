Travel Tales

Hoot, Scoot and Flicks on Route 66
by
Llewellyn M. Toulmin

Entering Arizona on the final leg of our Route 66 adventure, we encountered the fabulous Painted Desert and Petrified Forest National Parks. These wonders are located right next to each other in northeast Arizona. The Painted Desert has long vistas of red, orange and yellow rocks stretching off into the far distance. We were stunned to read on one marker that the tiny mountain range we could barely see on the horizon was 125 miles away.

By contrast, our focus in the Petrified Forest was up close and personal. Here we wandered amidst large fallen trees, crouched down to peer at them, and were fascinated to learn that all the wood had been replaced by colorful minerals 225 million years ago.

Driving further west in Arizona near the Grand Canyon, our classic 1968 “Bullitt” Mustang had its only real breakdown of the trip. At a gas station, suddenly the Mustang wouldn’t start. We had it towed 100 feet to another station, a dead starter motor was diagnosed, and we were on our way in two hours. Later in the trip the lights cut out briefly and then the points had to be replaced, both minor items. So, 5000 miles with three minor problems, not bad for a 40 year old car —which is 400 in people years!

As to the car’s “green” performance, we averaged a magnificent 12 miles per gallon most of the way, although this dropped inexplicably to just 4 miles per gallon at the start in Chicago. That car does not like Chicago. I was sweating bullets that we were only going to average 4 mpg the whole way—let’s see, 5000 miles at 4 mpg equals 1250 gallons times $4 per gallon high test (at the time) equals $5000! Yikes! Luckily, once we got out on the road and away from the city our average scooted up to 10 to 13 mpg, thank goodness. Yes, when we fire up that baby and head out on the highway, we don’t worry about global warming, we worry about Galactic Warming.

We detoured 80 miles south of Route 66 to Scottsdale and Phoenix. There we found that a remarkable car culture has sprung up. While most towns in the US are lucky to have a “classic car cruise” once a month in the spring and summer, Scottsdale has two huge informal car shows and cruises every week all year, at the Scottsdale Pavilions Mall. I guess if you put together a lot of male retirees, a lot of money, and a lot of dry desert air that preserves classic cars, you will get a pretty vigorous car scene. There is a reason why the famous Barrett-Jackson Car Auction originates in Scottsdale.

We wound through twisty roads and peered over dramatic dropoffs in western Arizona to reach the dusty little town of Oatman, Arizona. Oatman is famous for its wild burros that wander loose in the street, and for the fact that Clark Gable and Carol Lombard honeymooned in the Oatman Hotel. We looked for the burros and couldn’t find any, then tried to get into the honeymoon suite and found it was under reconstruction. We departed Oatman swiftly, wiping the evidence of the donkeys off the soles of our shoes, and wondering why any movie stars had ever decided to visit there, much less honeymoon.

That evening we stopped for dinner at the famous Baghdad Café in Newberry Springs, California. This café starred in the 1988 movie by the same name, starring C.C.H. Pounder and Jack Palance. The movie, about a German woman abandoned in the desert who transforms the Baghdad Café and its patrons, did not make a big splash in the US, but is very highly regarded in France, Germany and Austria. Thousands of visitors from those countries drive Route 66 every year just to visit this motion picture shrine. Generally we were surprised to find that many sites on Route 66 attract more foreign visitors, especially Europeans and Japanese, than Americans.

In California, we naturally scored big in the kooky character department. Elmer Long is the creator of the bottle ranch in Barstow, California. For the last nine years Elmer, a retired engineer, has labored ten hours a day, erecting thousands of bottle trees on his nine acre ranch. A bottle tree is a 10 to 20 foot length of pipe with rebar “branches” welded on. Elmer slips bottles, mostly antiques, some very rare and beautiful, onto the ends of the branches, and then “plants” the tree in a concrete base in the dirt.

The biggest hoot of our entire journey on Route 66 came near Elmer’s ranch. We had stopped at a Route 66 Museum that turned out to be closed, when we were suddenly surrounded by a tough looking gang of Hell’s Angels, complete with Harleys, leathers, tattoos and nose rings. Their leader began talking to us in a very odd accent, and said, “Vee are Finns from the Finland, and are wery, wery concerned about these fires of forest that CNN says are burning up all of the LA. Is it really safe to go into the LA on the Route 66?”

I said, “The fires are only in the Simi Valley, 20 miles north of LA. CNN always exaggerates everything, and anyway, California is always full of disasters. You should just calmly thread your way between them.” This advice did not seem to reassure the fearful Finns, who went into a powwow, one faction clearly terrified of being burned alive on Santa Monica Boulevard. Perhaps they are still stuck there, arguing in the parking lot? Naturally, the next day was beautiful with no trace of smoke as we cruised down Route 66 through downtown Los Angeles.


The end of Route 66
As Steve McQueen fans driving a McQueen-related movie car, we were fortunate to attend an auction of his memorabilia put on by Steve’s ex-wife, Neile McQueen Tofel at the Petersen Automotive Museum in Los Angeles, right on Route 66. McQueen letters, pictures, an honorary police badge, firearms, movie posters and movie props were all auctioned off by Bonhams, while Neile gave a live, in-person running commentary about her life with Steve. We were going to bid on a signed picture of Steve on the set of “Bullitt” that was estimated to go for $500, but when the bidding went up to $2400 in two minutes, I hid my bidding paddle under my bum, so the auctioneer couldn’t see it. Oh, well, we have a “Bullitt” car and the successful bidder doesn’t.

That night at sunset we reached the Pacific Ocean in Santa Monica and found the brass plaque that marks the end of Route 66. We drove the Mustang onto the San Monica pier, scene of countless movie shoots, and looked out at the blue Pacific, wishing the continent would go on and on, so we could continue our wonderful journey. It was not to be. But wait a minute—how about if we stored the car, came back next year, and drove north on the Pacific Coast Highway, all the way from Mexico to Canada? Hmmmm…
