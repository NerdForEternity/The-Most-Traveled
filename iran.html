Give Cars a Chance:

Americans Praise Iran During
Tough Peking to Paris Classic Car Challenge

by
Llewellyn Toulmin

While Washington and Teheran edged closer to conflict, a band of intrepid international and US classic car enthusiasts successfully negotiated a complex deal with Iranian officials, rallied their cars for five days across Iran, and enthused about their interactions with the Iranian people, as part of a larger Peking to Paris road rally. The rally took place from September 10 to October 16, 2010.

According to Phillip Young, head of Britain’s Endurance Rally Association, “Our Peking to Paris Motor Challenge successfully rallied across Iran and 19 other countries, with 107 teams, including eight from the USA and the balance from 25 other nations.” Stated Young, “This is the most international major motorsports event in the world – more so than Le Mans, Monte Carlo or Indianapolis – and probably the toughest. We had fourteen Americans on the Challenge and this fact caused no problems at all on the Iranian portion of the rally.”

Young elaborated on the complicated negotiations needed to allow the rally, with its 250 participants and support staff, to enter, cross and exit Iran. “The major issue in Iran as in every country is the heavy import duty, which must be paid if the car is left or sold in the country and doesn’t exit as promised. Normally a very large bond is posted, often five times the value of the car. But some of the Peking to Paris Challenge cars are worth more than a million dollars each, and the aggregate value of the 107 team and 16 support vehicles is many millions.” Young continued, “So we asked for a waiver, and were assured this was being processed. What we didn’t know was that behind the scenes one of the Iranian revenue agencies objected, and demanded the bond be posted by someone, if not the ERA. So the Touring and Automobile Club of Iran stepped in and posted the bond on our behalf, to ensure the event went forward.” Concluded Young, “We were very impressed with the ability of the Iranian government and society to be flexible, negotiate in good faith, and yet live within their rules and laws.”

The Challenge competitors were surprised and pleased with the friendliness of the Iranian people during the rally. Stated David Ayre of Berkshire, UK, driving the rally’s oldest car, a 1907 Italia 40, “We broke a key rear wheel part in Iran. A local factory owner took me to his factory at 11:00 pm, and he and his staff worked all night to machine the parts from scratch that we needed.”

David Staples of Orange County, California, driving a 1968 VW Beetle, had a similar experience. He said, “We were all nervous about Iran. But we stayed in very nice five star hotels. The local Iranian hot rod club showed up with some nice old Mustangs, a beautiful Caddy and a ’55 T-bird.” Said Staples, “My son Garrick in his 1959 VW Beetle Cabriolet had on-going mechanical problems on the horrible dirt track roads of Mongolia and Turkmenistan. He limped into Iran, where the local VW owners club took great care of him. They decided he had a serious engine problem, probably a broken connecting rod, so they located an entire new engine block, installed it overnight, and Garrick drove off the next morning!”

Fritz James of Iowa, navigator of a 1929 Ford Model A Phaeton, said, “At first in Iran we were afraid to say we were US citizens. But we were so stunned with the friendliness of the people and the beauty of the country that soon we were proudly announcing our nationality to everyone.” Continued James, “One old Iranian man came up to us and kept gesticulating. I finally asked , ‘What are you trying to say?’ He said, ‘I am trying to say: I love you!’”

The 2010 Peking to Paris Motor Challenge was the third running of the event in the last decade, commemorating the original 1907 road race. Rally vehicles included a 1926 Rolls Royce Silver Ghost, 1929 Studebaker President, 1922 FN motorbike and sidecar, and even a 1965 “007” Aston Martin DB5.

Each day’s rally usually involved 250 to 450 miles and 10 to 15 hours of driving, including several hours of rallying on dirt or gravel roads. Teams tackled some of the most difficult terrain and worst roads on the planet. Said Steve Hyde of the UK, winner of the 1922 to 1941 category in his 1937 Chevrolet “Fangio” coupe, “I climbed Kilimanjaro six months ago, and this rally is much tougher than that.”

The competitors’ goal was to follow the thousands of road, mileage or GPS rally instructions exactly, meet or go faster than the allotted time limits, and complete each daily section without breaking down or getting lost. Unannounced checkpoints ensured that each car was following the specified difficult route. Virtually every car suffered major mechanical difficulties, especially in Mongolia, which had just had its already rough dirt roads pounded by the same torrential rains that flooded Pakistan.

The rally had no cash prizes, only modest medals and trophies in the three age categories. Of course, all of the 85 cars that made the 37-day rally could rightly claim to have survived one of the toughest motorsport events ever conceived. And as Phillip Young said, “Who knows? Perhaps as in the era of ‘Ping-Pong diplomacy,’ each of us on the rally did a little bit to demonstrate that international conflict with Iran is not inevitable.”
