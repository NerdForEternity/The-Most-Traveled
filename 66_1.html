Gettingĺ Your Kicks on Route 66
by
Llewellyn M. Toulmin

Amazing history, kooky characters, beautiful scenery and surprising sights: Route 66 has it all!


The beginning of Route 66 in Chicago
My wife Susan and I began our Route 66 adventure at the absolute start of the road, at Michigan Avenue and Adams Street in Chicago, where a helpful sign says ôBegin Historic Illinois Route 66.ö But starting there is not easy. The street is one-way, during the day the traffic is horrendous, and the curb lane is a bus lane where cars are not allowed. So naturally, we drove in the bus lane, stopped, and got a picture of our steedŚa 1968 ôBullittö MustangŚbeneath the sign. Our secret: arrive at the start by 4:00 amŚno cops around then!

Whizzing southwest out of Chicago, we watched the sun rise over the beautiful misty fields of rural Illinois, as we drove on our first bit of authentic Route 66. The history of Route 66 is fascinating but complicated. It was not envisioned as a major road at first, as witnessed by its number. In 1926, when the route numbering system was launched, major coast to coast roads were given numbers ending in zero, similar to our modern interstates. So 66 was not originally planned as a major highway. But Route 66 became the first road to be paved all the way across the mid- and south-west through to Los Angeles. Thus it was the main migration route for people fleeing the Dust Bowl and the Depression, seeking a better life in California. It became famous as ôThe Mother Roadö in John Steinbeckĺs The Grapes of Wrath, and was popularized by the Bobby Troup/Nat King Cole song ôGet Your Kicks on Route 66ö in 1946, and the famous TV show ôRoute 66ö in the 1960s.

Oddly, while Route 66 was getting more attention in the popular culture, on the ground it was dying. The road by design went through the middles of hundreds of small towns as ôThe Main Street of America,ö so the often bumper-to-bumper cars headed west could get gas, food and accommodations. But the Interstate Highway System of the 1960s had a different philosophyŚbypass the towns. This unintentionally killed many of them off, and led to the de-commissioning of Route 66 as an official road in 1985.

But Route 66 refuses to die, and it is certainly one of the most famous roads in the world, even though it exists only as a state of mind. In many areas historic brown signs help the motorist find the original route, which now lies under other roads and Interstates. But if you buy the right guidebooks, especially the detailed EZ66 Guide by Jerry McClanahan, you can find the few bits of rural road that are the original alignments of the famous route.

Our first surprising sight on the Route was the gargantuan Gemini Giant at the Launching Pad Restaurant in Wilmington, Illinois. The Giant was originally a Muffler Man, designed to advertise auto mufflers. But in the 1960s the enterprising owners of the restaurant decided to alter him to advertise their Space Age restaurant. Similar giants dot the landscape along Route 66. (For a national listing of these giant fiberglass humanoids, see www.roadsideamerica.com/muffler.)


The Munger Moss Motel on Route 66
Food on the Route is generally pure AmericanaŚburgers, dogs and fries. We stopped for dinner at the Cozy Dog Inn in Springfield, Illinois, where corn dogs were actually invented in 1946, under the original name of ôCrusty Cur.ö Good PR to change that name! Later in our trip, our best meal on Route 66 was a true ôOh My God!ö burger experience at the Bobcat Bite restaurant southeast of Santa Fe, New Mexico. The place looks like a shack, but they use top grade organic meat, fresh ground every day, and patrons line up to get that great taste.

Leaving Illinois for Missouri, we stayed at a fascinating motel in the town of Lebanon. The Munger Moss Motel was named after two of its owners, and is famous for its huge neon sign and nostalgic accommodations. The sign is one of the few fully functioning remaining neon signs in AmericaŚthere were thousands in the 1950s but most are now gone. The accommodations are simple but clean, with small rooms opening onto a central ôcar court.ö That was what everyone stayed in back in the Ĺ50s. For $70 per night compared to an average $110 per night for our usual choice of Holiday Inn Express or Hampton Inn, it was a bargain.

Also in Missouri we found one of the most interesting characters on the Route. Artist, architect and preservationist Lowell Davis grew up in Red Oak, Missouri. His small hometown became a ghost town, so after making his fortune creating and selling small figurines, he decided to re-create his childhood memories. He purchased land 20 miles away, northeast of Carthage, and re-located most of the buildings from Red Oak, re-arranged them and restored them, to create his dream town of Red Oak II. His abode is his prize restoration, a double house composed of the house where the famous woman outlaw Belle Starr was born, and the house where Belle lived and was killed by her son for the reward money!

Davis keeps a low profile, and there is no advertising for this amazing little town that looks like it sprang full-blown from the early 1900s. The town is kooky but fascinatingŚan apt symbol for all of Route 66.

For more information on Route 66, see www.historic66.com. Lew Toulmin is a member of the North East Rally Club and has competed in his Mustang for thousands of miles in time/speed/distance rallies. He lives in Silver Spring.
