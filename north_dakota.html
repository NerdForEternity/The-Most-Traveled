ND: Not Your Dadĺs Dakota

by
Llewellyn Toulmin


A Lewis and Clark Trail sign marks the way to adventure
I finally made it to North Dakota, my fiftieth state visited. I expected a state with little history, flat boring dry terrain, a depressed economy with high unemployment, and a few little old farmers driving slow tractors.

I was wrong on all counts.

Twenty feet into North Dakota, having driven in from Montana, I ran right into history as fascinating as any in Maryland or Virginia. The Fort Union Trading Post was set up in 1828 by John Jacob Astorĺs American Fur Company. It was a private fort, set up by one of the most powerful companies in US history, which controlled all the commerce in the Dakotas, Montana and Wyoming until 1867. Located at the strategic confluence of the Yellowstone and Missouri rivers, the company obtained buffalo robes, beaver skins, otter and other furs from numerous Indian tribes, including the Assiniboine, Crow, Cree, Ojibway, Blackfoot and Hidatsa.

Today the re-created Fort is an imposing tourist attraction, with 18 foot high walls, two large stone towers and an impressive managerĺs house, now a museum. The Fort is 24 miles southwest of Williston, North Dakota, and is well worth a visit.

Further south, at the Knife River Indian Villages, I learned the history of one of Americaĺs most famous women, Sacagawea. Sacagawea (who probably pronounced her name as Sakakawea) was only sixteen when she and her trapper husband Toussaint Charbonneau joined the famous Lewis and Clark Corps of Discovery in 1805. Although born a Shoshone in Idaho, she was captured by her enemies and was living in the Knife River village 40 miles north of Bismarck, North Dakota when she set out for fame and fortune with her three month old baby on her back. She played a key role in guiding and translating for the Corps, made it back to the Knife River, then sadly died at the age of only 24. As the park ranger at Knife River said, ôSakakawea holds the record for being the American woman most written about, compared to what is actually known about her life.ö

The terrain in west and central North Dakota is anything but flat and boring. The Theodore Roosevelt National Park contains a surprisingly lovely canyon with dramatic formations almost 500 feet tall, all carved by the Little Missouri River. A thirteen mile scenic drive provides overlooks of the river, encounters with bison, and views of ôcannonball concretionsö ľ large round rocks five feet thick that fall out of the canyon walls.

The beautiful grasslands surrounding the canyon are named for Teddy Roosevelt, who hunted, ranched and matured in this area. ôI never would have become President without my experiences in North Dakota,ö he said, reflecting on how he learned to value people by their character and accomplishments, rather than by their wealth or social standing.


The Missouri River in flood.
The state was certainly not dry, at least when we visited in June 2011. Floods on the Missouri River almost prevented us from crossing to the south side of the state. Floods on the Souris River in Minot cut the town in two and forced a quarter of the population to evacuate. Standing water in soaked fields across the state killed off the crops and resulted in clouds of mosquitoes and gnats.

In yet another surprise, the economy in North Dakota is booming, driven by the biggest oil strike in recent US history. Known as the Bakken, the oil shale deposit covers large parts of North Dakota, Montana and south central Canada. The US Geological Survey estimated in 2008 that the strike contains 4.3 billion barrels of oil. But recent estimates by local experts put the figure at about 20 to 24 billion barrels. As a result, North Dakota will likely become one of the top fifteen oil producing jurisdictions in the world, ranking higher than Libya, Algeria or Brazil. (Incidentally, the USA is the third largest oil producer in the world ľ bet you didnĺt know that.) Because of the decline in US demand for oil caused by the Great Recession, and the increase in domestic production from the Bakken, President Obamaĺs announced goal of reducing oil imports by one third by 2025 is probably achievable and may even be beatable.

The North Dakota oil patch economy is so hot that last year, 1213 new wells were drilled, there are 17,000 oil jobs unfilled and begging for workers, oil roustabouts are living in tents and in RVs parked at Walmarts because there is not enough housing, and ôman campsö (movable dormitories) are being constructed at a rapid rate.

Instead of little old farmers driving tractors, the North Dakota roads are choked with huge oil trucks and movable drilling rigs, most of them marked ôWide Load,ö driving at high speeds and scaring the locals ľ and me ľ to death.

All in all, North Dakota may not be the most beautiful or historic state in the union, but I think it certainly qualifies as the most surprising.

Lew Toulmin lives in Silver Spring and has traveled to all 50 states and 137 of the worldĺs 194 countries.
